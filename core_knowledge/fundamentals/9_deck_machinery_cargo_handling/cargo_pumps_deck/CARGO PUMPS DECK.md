# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Deck Pumping

## 1.1 The Physics of Suction Lift (The 7-Meter Limit)
Unlike deepwell pumps, deck-mounted pumps must "pull" the cargo up from the tank.
*   **The Physics:** Suction is created by generating a vacuum. Atmospheric pressure then pushes the cargo up the pipe.
*   **The Limit:** Theoretical maximum lift for water is 10.3m. In practice, due to pump efficiency and cargo vapor pressure, the maximum lift is **approx. 7 meters**. If the tank is deeper than this, the pump will **Cavitate** and flow will stop.

## 1.2 Vapor Pressure and Boiling Physics
*   **The Physics:** High vapor pressure cargos (like Gasoline or Methanol) "boil" at lower vacuums. 
*   **Effect:** If the pump suction creates a vacuum that matches the cargo's vapor pressure, the cargo turns to gas in the pipe. The pump becomes **Vapor Locked**.

## 1.3 Hazardous Zone Physics (Ex-Proof)
Deck pumps operate in "Zone 0" or "Zone 1" environments where explosive gasses are always present.
*   **The Physics:** Any electrical or mechanical spark is an ignition source. 
*   **Drive Solution:** Most deck pumps are driven by **Hydraulics** or **Compressed Air** because they are inherently "Intrinsically Safe." If electric motors are used, they must be "Flame-proof" (Ex-d).

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Pump Unit
Usually centrifugal or screw-type. 
*   **Portable Pumps:** Compact units that can be lowered into a tank or bolted to a deck manifold in an emergency.

## 2.2 Drive System (Hydraulic/Pneumatic)
*   **Hydraulic Jumper Hoses:** Connect the pump to the ship's main HPU (Hydraulic Power Unit). Use "Snap-tite" or "Aeroquip" quick-connect couplings.
*   **Air Motors:** Used for smaller stripping pumps. Relies on the ship's Working Air system.

## 2.3 Mechanical Seals and Cooling
*   **Physics:** Pumping cargo generates friction heat at the seal. 
*   **Cooling:** Many deck pumps use the cargo itself or a dedicated "Seal Flush" line to cool the mechanical seal. **Warning:** If the pump runs dry, the seal will explode from heat in seconds.

## 2.4 Cargo Hoses and Manifolds
*   **Hose Physics:** Must be chemically compatible with the cargo. Reinforced with stainless steel wire to handle the discharge pressure (typically 10 bar).

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Prime:** The pump should establish flow within 2 minutes of starting.
*   **Vibration:** Minimal. High vibration indicates cavitation or a bent shaft (often from rough handling).
*   **Couplings:** No oil leaks from the hydraulic jumper hoses.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Discharge Pressure** | 4.0 – 8.0 bar | > 10.0 bar (Hose Limit) |
| **Hydraulic Drive Press**| 100 – 180 bar | > 210 bar (Relief Valve) |
| **Air Supply (Pneumatic)**| 6.0 – 7.0 bar | < 5.0 bar (Stalling) |
| **Max Suction Lift** | < 6 meters | > 7 meters (Cavitation) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Cavitation (The "Gravel" Sound)
*   **Symptom:** Loud rattling; discharge pressure gauge fluctuates wildly.
*   **Root Cause:** Pumping too fast at low tank levels or air leaking into the suction hose joints.

## 4.2 Mechanical Seal Failure (The Leak)
*   **Symptom:** Cargo spraying from the pump gland.
*   **Root Cause:** Running the pump dry or abrasive particles (sand/rust) in the cargo.
*   **Action:** Stop immediately! Cargo on deck is a massive fire and environmental hazard.

## 4.3 Hydraulic "Kick" (Water Hammer)
*   **Symptom:** Sudden "Bang" in the hoses when starting or stopping.
*   **Root Cause:** Opening the hydraulic control valve too quickly.
*   **Physics:** Hydraulic oil is incompressible. A sudden stop creates a massive pressure spike that can burst a hose.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Prime Assist" Trick
*   **Trade Trick:** If a deck pump won't prime because the suction lift is too high, **partially fill the discharge hose with cargo from the manifold** (Back-filling). This "wets" the impeller and helps create the initial vacuum.

## 5.2 Checking Coupling "O-Rings"
*   **Trade Trick:** If the hydraulic motor is weak, check the quick-connect couplings. **Expert Insight:** Often, a tiny rubber O-ring has snapped and is partially blocking the flow of oil. Replace the O-ring and the power will return.

## 5.3 The "Ice-Block" Trick for Air Motors
*   **Trade Trick:** Pneumatic air motors often "Freeze Up" in humid weather due to the expanding air cooling the exhaust. **Solution:** Spray a little **Antifreeze or Alcohol** into the air inlet. It lowers the freezing point of the moisture and keeps the motor running.

## 5.4 Using "Jumper Hoses" as a Diagnostic Tool
*   **Expert Insight:** If you suspect a pump is failing, swap the hydraulic jumper hoses with a known "good" pump. If the fault stays with the pump, the **Mechanical Seal or Impeller** is bad. If the fault moves, the **HPU or Control Valve** is bad.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Pre-Cargo Checklist
*   **Hose Continuity:** Use a Multimeter to check the electrical resistance of the cargo hose. It must be **< 10 Ohms** to ensure static electricity is grounded.
*   **Pressure Test:** Visually inspect hoses for "bulges" or "weeping."

## 6.2 Post-Cargo Cleaning
*   **Method:** Always flush the pump with Fresh Water or a compatible solvent after use. **Expert Insight:** If you leave "Drying Oils" (like Palm Oil) or chemicals in the pump, they will harden and seize the impeller before the next port.

## 6.3 Hydraulic Hose Care
*   **Method:** Store hydraulic hoses in a dark locker when not in use. **Sunlight (UV)** destroys the rubber. Cap the ends to prevent salt and dirt from entering the hydraulic system.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Emergency Stripping:** Portable pumps are often used to "strip" the last 100 liters from a tank that the main cargo pumps cannot reach.
*   **Static Bonding:** Always connect the "Grounding Cable" (Bonding wire) between the portable pump and the ship's hull before starting. The friction of the flowing cargo can generate **30,000 Volts** of static electricity.
