# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Lifting

## 1.1 The Physics of SWL (Safe Working Load)
A marine crane is a balanced lever system designed to move heavy loads between the ship and the pier.
*   **The Physics:** Torque $M = F \cdot d$. 
    The "Overturning Moment" created by the load at the jib tip must be balanced by the weight of the crane housing and the strength of the "Slewing Bearing." 
*   **Dynamic Load Physics:** Unlike shore cranes, marine cranes must handle **Dynamic Loading** caused by the ship's roll and heave. A 20-ton container can effectively weigh 30 tons if the ship drops suddenly in a swell.

## 1.2 The "Load-Radius" Relationship
*   **The Physics:** As the jib (boom) lowers (increasing radius), the overturning moment increases.
*   **Safety Limit:** Most cranes have an automatic "Moment Limiter." If you try to lift a maximum load at a maximum radius, the crane will automatically stop "Luffing Down" to prevent the jib from buckling or the bearing from ripping.

## 1.3 Hydraulic vs. Electric Physics
1.  **Hydraulic Cranes:** Use high-pressure oil (up to 300 bar). **Physics:** Incompressible fluid provides very precise control and high "Holding Torque."
2.  **Electric (VFD) Cranes:** Use frequency control. **Physics:** More energy efficient and eliminates the risk of oil spills on cargo.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Slewing Bearing (Pedestal)
The most critical mechanical part. It carries the entire weight of the crane and the load.
*   **Physics:** It must handle axial compression, radial shear, and a massive "Tilting Moment."

## 2.2 Hoist, Luff, and Slew Winches
1.  **Hoist:** Raises and lowers the hook.
2.  **Luff:** Raises and lowers the jib (boom).
3.  **Slew:** Rotates the crane 360°.

## 2.3 The Hydraulic Power Unit (HPU)
Located inside the crane housing. Consists of a large electric motor driving multiple variable-displacement pumps.

## 2.4 Wire Ropes and Sheaves
*   **Physics:** The wire rope diameter and "Lay" are matched to the SWL. Sheaves (pulleys) increase mechanical advantage (e.g., a 4-fall system reduces the line tension by 4x).

## 2.5 Safety Devices (Limit Switches)
*   **Slack Wire Switch:** Stops the hoist if the rope goes loose.
*   **Top Limit:** Prevents the hook from hitting the jib head.
*   **Load Cell:** Measures the actual weight of the cargo.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Smoothness:** The jib should move without "Jerking." Slewing should be silent.
*   **Brakes:** The load should stay perfectly still when the controls are in neutral (zero "creep").
*   **Oil:** Hydraulic oil should be clear and cool (below 60°C).

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Max Radius SWL** | 20 – 40 Tons | Varies by Boom Angle |
| **Hydraulic Pressure** | 200 – 300 bar | > 320 bar (Relief Valve) |
| **Slewing Speed** | 0.5 – 1.0 RPM | < 0.3 RPM (Motor Wear) |
| **Oil Temperature** | 45°C – 55°C | > 75°C (Shutdown) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 "Creeping" Load
*   **Symptom:** The hook slowly sinks down when the operator releases the handle.
*   **Root Cause:** Leaking internal seals in the "Counterbalance Valve" or worn winch brake pads.
*   **Danger:** A creeping load can cause a serious accident during container positioning.

## 4.2 Slewing Bearing "Rocking"
*   **Symptom:** The entire crane housing "Tilts" or "Clunks" when picking up a load.
*   **Root Cause:** Worn ball bearings or loose pedestal bolts.
*   **Physics:** If the bearing clearance exceeds approx. 2-3mm, the bearing is failing.

## 4.3 High Hydraulic Temperature
*   **Symptom:** "Oil Temp High" alarm after only 1 hour of cargo work.
*   **Root Cause:** Clogged oil cooler (air-side) or a pump that is "Bypassing" internally (generating heat instead of flow).

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Grease Sample" Test (Slewing Bearing)
*   **Trade Trick:** To check the health of the slewing bearing, take a sample of the used grease from the bearing seals. **If the grease contains shiny steel flakes, the bearing races are flaking (Spalling).** The bearing must be replaced (a multi-day shipyard job).

## 5.2 The "Rocking Gauge" Measurement
*   **Method:** Place a dial gauge between the crane housing and the pedestal. Pick up a maximum load. The "Vertical Play" is the rocking clearance. **Expert Insight:** Record this every 6 months to track the wear rate.

## 5.3 Emergency "Manual Lowering"
*   **Trade Trick:** If the power fails with a container in the air, you can manually lower it. **Method:** Locate the manual override on the "Brake Solenoid" and the "Proportional Valve." **Warning:** Open the brake only 1% to allow the load to "Ooze" down. If you open it too far, you cannot stop it.

## 5.4 Detecting "Slack Wire" Faults
*   **Expert Insight:** If the hoist won't work, check the "Slack Wire" switch at the winch drum. Often, the wire has "Jumped" a groove and is pushing the switch. **Trade Trick:** Re-spool the wire under tension using a piece of timber to guide it.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine
*   **Wire Inspection:** Check for "Broken Wires." If you find > 10 broken wires in 1 diameter of length, the rope must be condemned.
*   **Greasing:** The "Slewing Bearing" requires grease while the crane is **rotating**. This ensures even distribution across the races.

## 6.2 Annual Load Test (Mandatory)
*   **Requirement:** Perform a test lift at **110% of SWL** (e.g., 44 tons for a 40-ton crane). The crane must hold the load for 5 minutes and perform all motions.
*   **Record:** Update the "Register of Lifting Appliances" (The Chain Register).

## 6.3 Hydraulic Filter Change
*   **Method:** Change filters every 1000 hours. Crane hydraulics are extremely sensitive to dirt. A single grain of sand can jam a proportional valve spool.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Sea-Fastening:** Always engage the "Jib Rest" and the "Slew Lock Pin" before going to sea. The inertia of a loose jib in heavy weather can snap the slewing bearing.
*   **Wind Limits:** Most cranes are prohibited from working if the wind speed exceeds **20 meters/sec** (approx. 40 knots) because the "Wind Surface" of a container becomes uncontrollable.
