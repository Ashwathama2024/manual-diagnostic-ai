# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Anchoring

## 1.1 The Physics of Ground Tackle
A windlass doesn't just hold the anchor; it manages the entire "Ground Tackle" system.
*   **The Physics of the Catenary:** The holding power of an anchor comes primarily from the **Weight of the Chain** lying on the seabed, not just the anchor fluke. The curve formed by the suspended chain is a "Catenary." 
*   **Static vs. Dynamic Load:** The windlass must handle the static weight of the anchor and chain plus the dynamic loads from the ship's "Surge" and "Heave" in a seaway.

## 1.2 Brake Holding Physics
The brake is the most critical safety component.
*   **The Physics:** The brake must be able to hold **80% of the Minimum Breaking Load (MBL)** of the anchor chain. 
*   **Coefficient of Friction ($\mu$):** The brake relies on the friction between the asbestos-free lining and the steel drum. If the drum is rusty or oily, $\mu$ drops, and the anchor will "Run Away."

## 1.3 Motor Torque and Power Physics
*   **The Physics:** The windlass motor must provide enough torque to lift the anchor and **at least 3 shackles (approx. 82m)** of chain from the seabed at a speed of approx. 9 meters/minute.
*   **Hydraulic Power:** High-pressure hydraulic systems (up to 250 bar) provide high starting torque, which is essential for "breaking out" an anchor stuck in deep mud.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Wildcat (Gypsy)
A specially shaped sprocket wheel that matches the dimensions of the chain links. 
*   **Physics:** It must ensure that at least **180° of chain wrap** is maintained to prevent the chain from "jumping" under load.

## 2.2 The Friction Brake (Band Brake)
A steel band lined with friction material that wraps around the wildcat drum.
*   **Actuation:** Can be manual (Handwheel) or hydraulic (Remote). **Physics:** The "Toggle Linkage" provides mechanical advantage, multiplying the handwheel force into tons of clamping pressure.

## 2.3 The Dog Clutch (Coupling)
Allows the motor to be disconnected from the wildcat.
*   **Function:** Disengaged for "Letting Go" the anchor under gravity. Engaged for "Heaving In."

## 2.4 Spurling Pipe and Chain Locker
*   **Spurling Pipe:** The vertical pipe through which the chain travels from the windlass to the locker.
*   **Chain Locker:** A deep compartment where the chain is stored. **Physics:** The "Bitter End" is the last link, secured to the ship's structure with a quick-release pin.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Heaving:** Smooth movement with no "Jerking." The hydraulic motor sound is consistent.
*   **Braking:** The handwheel feels "Firm" when tightening. No squealing when the anchor is held.
*   **Alignment:** The chain travels centrally through the hawse pipe and wildcat.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Heaving Speed** | 9 – 12 m / min | < 6 m / min (Low Torque) |
| **Hydraulic Pressure** | 150 – 250 bar | > 280 bar (Relief Valve) |
| **Brake Lining Thick.** | > 50% of Original | < 2mm (Replace) |
| **Motor Current (Elec)** | < Rated Amps | High Amps (Stuck Anchor) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Brake "Fading" (Running Away)
*   **Symptom:** Anchor continues to pay out even when the brake is fully tight.
*   **Root Cause:** Worn linings, glazed drum surface, or oil/grease on the friction material.
*   **Danger:** A runaway anchor can rip the "Bitter End" out and lose the entire chain.

## 4.2 Wildcat "Jumping"
*   **Symptom:** Chain makes a loud "Bang" and jumps out of the wildcat pockets.
*   **Root Cause:** Worn wildcat pockets or the "Chain Stopper" is not aligned correctly.
*   **Physics:** The pockets have worn so much that the "Pitch" of the wheel no longer matches the chain links.

## 4.3 Hydraulic Motor "Stalling"
*   **Symptom:** Motor hums but won't turn while heaving.
*   **Root Cause:** Internal bypass in the motor or the anchor is "Fouled" (caught on a cable or rock).

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Chalk Test" for Brakes
*   **Trade Trick:** To check if the brake is holding evenly, rub white chalk across the entire width of the brake drum. Tighten and release the brake. **If the chalk is only rubbed off in small spots, the brake band is warped** and needs "Re-shaping."

## 5.2 Detecting a "Glazed" Drum
*   **Expert Insight:** If the brake is tight but "Squeals" and slips slightly, the drum surface is "Glazed" (polished to a mirror finish). **Trade Trick:** Use a coarse abrasive disc or a file to "Scuff" the drum surface. This restores the coefficient of friction $(\mu)$.

## 5.3 Emergency "Anchor Release"
*   **Critical Action:** If the ship is dragging anchor towards a reef and the windlass is dead, you must "Slip the Anchor." 
*   **Method:** Go to the chain locker and release the **Bitter End pin**. Let the entire chain run out. **Safety:** Ensure everyone is clear of the deck; the speed of the chain will be lethal.

## 5.4 Using the "Stopper" to Save the Brake
*   **Expert Insight:** The windlass brake is for *handling*, not for *long-term holding*. **Trade Trick:** Once the anchor is set, always engage the **Guillotine Stopper (Bow Stopper)**. This transfers the load directly to the ship's hull and protects the windlass bearings and brake.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine
*   **Greasing:** Grease all 20+ grease nipples. Windlasses are exposed to constant salt spray; without grease, the bushings will seize in weeks.
*   **Operation:** Run the motor for 5 minutes in both directions to circulate the hydraulic oil.

## 6.2 Annual Brake Rendering Test
*   **Method:** Tighten the brake to the calculated torque. Use a hydraulic jack to try and "turn" the wildcat. The brake must hold up to its rated capacity. **This is a mandatory Class requirement.**

## 6.3 Chain Calibration
*   **Check:** Measure the diameter of the chain links. If the diameter has worn by **more than 10%**, the chain must be replaced. Worn chain will not fit the wildcat pockets correctly.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Shackle Marking:** Chain shackles are marked with "Kenter Shackles" and painted (White/Red/Yellow) so the bridge knows how much chain is out (e.g., "3 shackles in the water").
*   **Hawse Pipe Wash:** Always use the high-pressure water spray (Hawse pipe wash) when heaving in. Mud in the chain locker creates toxic $H_2S$ gas and increases the weight of the chain.
