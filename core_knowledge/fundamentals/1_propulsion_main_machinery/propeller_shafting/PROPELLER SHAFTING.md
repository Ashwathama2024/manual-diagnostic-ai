# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Power Transmission

## 1.1 Torque and Thrust Physics
The propeller shafting line must perform two primary physical functions:
1.  **Torque Transmission:** Transferring the rotational energy (Torque, $Q$) from the engine/gearbox to the propeller.
    \[ P = 2 \pi n Q \]
    Where $P$ is power and $n$ is rotational speed. The shaft must resist torsional fatigue and "whirling" vibrations.
2.  **Axial Thrust Transmission:** Transferring the forward/aft force (Thrust, $T$) generated by the propeller to the ship's hull via the **Thrust Bearing**.

## 1.2 Shaft Alignment Physics
The shafting line must be perfectly aligned to ensure uniform load distribution across all bearings.
*   **The Physics of Deflection:** A long shaft is not a rigid beam; it behaves elastically. As the ship's hull flexes due to loading (cargo) and sea state, the bearing supports move.
*   **Thermal Expansion:** As the engine and gearbox heat up, they expand vertically, shifting the alignment. This "Cold vs. Hot" offset must be calculated during installation.

## 1.3 Stern Tube Physics
The tail shaft passes through the hull via the stern tube. The physics of the stern tube bearing (usually white-metal or synthetic) relies on **Hydrodynamic Lubrication**. As the shaft rotates, a "wedge" of oil is drawn between the shaft and the bearing, lifting the shaft and preventing metal-to-metal contact.

# Part 2 – Major Components & Systems

## 2.1 Intermediate and Tail Shafts
*   **Intermediate Shaft:** Connects the engine/gearbox to the tail shaft. Supported by independent "Plummer Blocks" (Intermediate Bearings).
*   **Tail Shaft (Propeller Shaft):** The final section that carries the propeller. It is often "cladded" with stainless steel or bronze in the seal areas to prevent corrosion.
*   **Coupling Bolts:** Precision-fitted or "Hydraulic Bolts" (e.g., Morgrip) that ensure the shaft sections act as a single unit.

## 2.2 Bearings
*   **Intermediate Bearings:** Typically white-metal lined, ring-lubricated or pressure-lubricated. They carry only the radial weight of the shaft.
*   **Thrust Bearing:** Usually integrated into the engine bedplate or gearbox. Uses "Tilting Pads" (Mitchell/Kingsbury type) to absorb the massive axial force of the propeller.
*   **Stern Tube Bearings:** Can be oil-lubricated (white metal) or water-lubricated (Thordon/Rubber).

## 2.3 Shaft Seals (Stern Tube Seals)
The most critical barrier between the engine room and the ocean.
*   **Lip Seals (Simplex Type):** Multiple rubber rings (Viton) that "hug" the shaft.
*   **Face Seals:** Uses a rotating carbon/silicon carbide face against a stationary seat.
*   **The Air-Guard System:** Modern seals use a small air cushion between the oil and water chambers to ensure zero oil leakage into the sea (Environmentally Acceptable Lubricant - EAL compliant).

## 2.4 Shaft Earthing Device (Grounding)
Because the rotating shaft is insulated by the oil film in the bearings, it can build up **Static Electricity** (Galvanic potential). 
*   **Component:** Silver-graphite brushes that rub against a slip ring on the shaft, grounding it to the hull. This prevents "spark erosion" (pitting) of the bearing surfaces.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Bearing Temperatures:** Stable and within 10°C of each other.
*   **Vibration:** Smooth rotation with no audible "thumping" (which indicates misalignment or a bent shaft).
*   **Seals:** Zero visible leakage in the engine room and stable oil level in the header tank.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Intermediate Bearing Temp** | 45°C – 60°C | > 75°C (High Temp) |
| **Stern Tube Oil Temp** | 40°C – 55°C | > 65°C (Bearing Distress) |
| **Shaft Earth Potential** | < 50 mV | > 80 mV (Brush Wear) |
| **Header Tank Level** | Stable | Rapid Drop (Seal Leak) |
| **Seal Oil Pressure** | Draft + 0.3 bar | < Draft (Water Ingress) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Bearing "Wipe" (Failure)
*   **Symptom:** Rapid rise in bearing temperature and "white metal" particles in the oil sample.
*   **Root Cause:** Loss of lubrication (pump failure), extreme misalignment, or water contamination of the oil.
*   **Physics:** Metal-to-metal contact generates localized heat exceeding the melting point of the tin-based white metal (approx. 230°C).

## 4.2 Seal Failure
*   **Symptom:** Water in the stern tube oil (milky appearance) or oil in the bilge.
*   **Root Cause:** Fishing nets/ropes entangled around the shaft, cutting the rubber lips.
*   **Expert Insight:** Excessive "Shaft Run-out" (wobble) due to worn bearings will cause the seals to fail prematurely as they cannot follow the shaft's eccentric movement.

## 4.3 Shaft Vibration (Whirling)
*   **Symptom:** Heavy vibration at specific RPMs (Critical Speeds).
*   **Root Cause:** Resonance. If the engine RPM matches the natural frequency of the shafting line.
*   **Correction:** Use a "Barred Speed Range" (BSR) where the engine is prohibited from running continuously.

## 4.4 Spark Erosion (Pitting)
*   **Symptom:** Microscopic pitting on the bearing journals and thrust pads.
*   **Root Cause:** Failed shaft earthing brushes. The potential difference between shaft and hull "jumps" across the oil film, taking metal with it.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Screwdriver Stethoscope"
*   **Trade Trick:** To check a noisy intermediate bearing, place a long screwdriver against the bearing housing and your ear. A "hum" is normal; a "rhythm" indicates a bearing shell that has moved; a "grind" indicates grit in the oil.

## 5.2 Measuring "Weardown" (The Poker Gauge)
How do you know if the underwater stern tube bearing is worn without drydocking?
*   **Method:** Use a **Poker Gauge** at the aft end of the stern tube (requires a diver or a drydock). It measures the vertical drop of the shaft. Wear > 1.5mm – 2.0mm usually requires bearing replacement.

## 5.3 Calculating Seal Oil Pressure "In Your Head"
*   **Trade Trick:** Every 1 meter of seawater is approx. 0.1 bar. If the ship's draft is 10 meters, the water pressure is 1 bar. Your seal oil header tank must be at least 13 meters above the shaft to maintain the +0.3 bar safety margin.

## 5.4 Detecting Water in Oil (The Crackle Test)
*   **Trade Trick:** Take an oil sample and drop a few drops on a hot plate (150°C). If it "crackles" or "pops," there is water in the oil. If it just smokes quietly, the oil is dry.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Daily / Watchkeeping Checks
*   **Visual:** Check the shaft earthing brushes for sparking and length.
*   **Temperature:** Feel the intermediate bearings (carefully!) for hot spots.
*   **Leakage:** Check the "Aft Seal Drain Tank." A few drops an hour is normal; a steady stream is a failure.

## 6.2 Oil Analysis (Quarterly)
Crucial for stern tube health.
*   **Water Content:** Must be < 0.1%.
*   **Chromium/Iron:** Indicates wear of the shaft liner or gears.

## 6.3 Coupling Bolt Inspection
During major overhauls, check for "Freting" (red dust) around the shaft couplings, which indicates the bolts are loose and the shaft sections are rubbing against each other.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **BSR (Barred Speed Range):** This is a range of RPMs (e.g., 45–55 RPM) where the engine is not allowed to stay. The ECS will "skip" through this range quickly during acceleration to avoid resonance damage to the shaft.
*   **Environmentally Acceptable Lubricants (EAL):** US EPA VGP regulations require ships in US waters to use biodegradable oil in the stern tube if there is a risk of sea-water interface.
