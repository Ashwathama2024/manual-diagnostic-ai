# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Pressure Maintenance

## 1.1 The Physics of the Compressed Air Cushion
Because water is incompressible, a pump alone cannot maintain a stable pressure in a ship's piping system without cycling on and off every time a tap is opened.
*   **The Physics:** The hydrophore tank contains a "buffer" of **Compressed Air** above the water.
*   **Boyle’s Law:** $P_1 V_1 = P_2 V_2$. 
    When water is pumped into the tank, the air volume $(V)$ decreases and the pressure $(P)$ increases. When a tap is opened, the compressed air expands, "pushing" the water out and maintaining pressure without needing the pump to start immediately.

## 1.2 "Water Logging" Physics
*   **The Problem:** Air is slightly soluble in water. Over time, the air cushion is absorbed into the water and carried away through the taps.
*   **Result:** The air volume decreases until the tank is 100% full of water. Since water is incompressible, the pump will "short-cycle" (starting and stopping every few seconds), which will burn out the motor.

## 1.3 Static Head vs. Pump Pressure
*   **The Physics:** The hydrophore must provide enough pressure to push water to the highest tap (usually the Bridge or Captain's cabin). 
*   **Rule of Thumb:** Every 10 meters of height requires 1.0 bar of pressure. If the Captain's cabin is 20m above the tank, the system must maintain at least 2.5 – 3.0 bar to ensure a "good flow" in the shower.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Hydrophore Tank
A pressure vessel designed to hold both water and compressed air. 
*   **Fittings:** Sight glass (to see the air/water level), pressure gauge, and safety relief valve.

## 2.2 Duty and Standby Pumps
Usually multi-stage centrifugal pumps.
*   **Control:** A **Pressure Switch** starts the pump at the "Cut-in" pressure (e.g., 3.5 bar) and stops it at the "Cut-out" pressure (e.g., 5.5 bar).

## 2.3 Air Charging Line
A connection from the ship's "Working Air" system to manually or automatically replenish the air cushion.

## 2.4 Water Treatment (Hygiene)
*   **UV Sterilizer:** Kills bacteria by damaging their DNA with ultraviolet light.
*   **Chlorinator:** Adds a controlled amount of chlorine to prevent biofilm growth in the pipes.
*   **Calcite Filter:** Neutralizes the acidic (low pH) water produced by the FWG to prevent pipe corrosion.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Air/Water Level:** The sight glass shows the water level at approximately 1/3 to 1/2 of the tank height when the pump stops.
*   **Cycling:** The pumps should not start more than 4–6 times per hour during normal usage.
*   **Quality:** Water is clear, odorless, and has a slight chlorine taste (approx. 0.2 – 0.5 ppm residual).

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **System Pressure** | 3.5 – 5.5 bar | < 2.5 bar (Low Press) |
| **Air Cushion Level** | 50% of Tank | > 90% (Water Logged) |
| **UV Lamp Status** | "Healthy" (Green) | "Fail" (Amber) |
| **Chlorine Residual** | 0.2 – 0.5 ppm | < 0.1 ppm (Bacteria Risk) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Water Logging (Short-Cycling)
*   **Symptom:** Pump starts/stops rapidly every time a tap is used.
*   **Root Cause:** Air cushion has been absorbed into the water or there is a small air leak from the top of the tank.

## 4.2 Low Pressure at High Taps
*   **Symptom:** Bridge taps only "dribble" water.
*   **Root Cause:** Pressure switch setpoints are too low, or the multi-stage pump impellers are worn and cannot reach the "Cut-out" pressure.

## 4.3 High Salinity / Bad Taste
*   **Symptom:** Water tastes salty or metallic.
*   **Root Cause:** FWG salinometer failure (salty water) or the mineralizer/calcite filter is exhausted (acidic water).

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 Recharging the Air Cushion (The "Manual Blow")
*   **Trade Trick:** If the tank is water-logged, don't just add air. Stop the pumps, **drain the water down to 1/4 level** using the drain valve, then add compressed air until the pressure reaches the "Cut-in" setpoint. This ensures a "deep" air cushion that will last for weeks.

## 5.2 The "Snift Valve" Check
*   **Expert Insight:** Some older systems have a "snift valve" on the pump suction that sucks in a tiny amount of air with every stroke. If this valve is blocked, the tank will water-log much faster. Clean the snift valve needle with a fine wire.

## 5.3 Checking the "Non-Return" Valve (NRV)
*   **Trade Trick:** If the hydrophore pressure drops rapidly when no one is using water, the NRV at the pump discharge is likely leaking. The pressure is "leaking back" into the fresh water storage tank.

## 5.4 Cleaning the UV Quartz Sleeve
*   **Trade Trick:** The UV lamp is inside a quartz tube. If this tube gets "filthy" with scale, the UV light cannot reach the water. **Wipe the quartz sleeve with alcohol** every month to ensure 100% sterilization.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Daily Routine
*   **Level Check:** Verify the air/water interface in the sight glass.
*   **Chlorine Test:** Use a colorimetric test kit to check the residual chlorine at the furthest tap (the Bridge).

## 6.2 Annual Tank Inspection
*   **Requirement:** The hydrophore tank is a pressure vessel. It must be opened, cleaned, and inspected for internal corrosion every 12 months. **Check the internal coating** (usually food-grade epoxy) for blistering.

## 6.3 Pump Seal Replacement
*   **Check:** Hydrophore pumps run frequently. If the mechanical seal drips, water will enter the motor bearings, causing a "Short Circuit." Replace the seal immediately if a leak is detected.
