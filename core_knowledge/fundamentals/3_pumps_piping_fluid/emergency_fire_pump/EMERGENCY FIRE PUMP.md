# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Emergency Supply

## 1.1 The Physics of Independence (SOLAS Regulation 10)
The emergency fire pump must be able to fight a fire in the Engine Room (ER) even if the main power and main fire pumps are lost.
*   **The Physics of Separation:** It must be located in a separate compartment (usually in the Steering Gear Room or a dedicated "Emergency Pump Room" forward).
*   **Independent Suction:** It must have its own dedicated sea chest and suction piping, completely separate from the main engine room sea chests.
*   **Static Lift:** Since the pump is often located higher in the ship than the main pumps, it must have a high-capacity **Self-Priming** system to overcome the increased static lift.

## 1.2 Prime Mover Physics (Stored Energy)
The pump must start without any ship's main power.
*   **Physics:** Most use a dedicated **Diesel Engine** with its own starting batteries or a hydraulic/compressed-air "Black Start" system. 
*   **Fuel Physics:** The fuel tank must contain enough fuel for **at least 3 hours** of continuous operation (SOLAS requirement).

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Pump Unit
Usually a high-head centrifugal pump. 
*   **Material:** Bronze or high-grade stainless steel to resist corrosion from stagnant seawater in the suction line.

## 2.2 The Diesel Engine (Prime Mover)
*   **Starting:** Electric (dual batteries), Hydraulic (hand-pumped accumulator), or Hand-cranked.
*   **Cooling:** Usually air-cooled or fresh-water cooled with a dedicated radiator to avoid dependency on the ship's SW cooling system.

## 2.3 The Priming System
*   **Exhaust Ejector:** Uses the diesel engine's exhaust gas flow to create a vacuum in the suction line (Venturi effect).
*   **Electric Priming Pump:** A small independent pump used to fill the main casing.

## 2.4 Fire Main Isolation Valve
A critical valve located between the Emergency Fire Pump and the Main Fire Pump. It allows the emergency pump to pressurize the rest of the ship while isolated from a leaking/damaged main in the Engine Room.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Start-up:** Engine fires on the first or second attempt (even in cold weather).
*   **Pressure:** Steady at 4.0 – 6.0 bar at the highest hydrant.
*   **Suction:** Vacuum builds quickly and water is delivered within 1 minute.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Engine Speed (Diesel)** | 1800 – 3000 RPM | Overspeed Trip |
| **Pump Pressure** | 4.0 – 7.0 bar | < 3 bar (Poor Throw) |
| **Battery Voltage** | 24.5V – 27.5V | < 22V (Start Blocked) |
| **Fuel Tank Level** | 100% (always) | < 50% (Refill Needed) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 "Battery Dead" (The #1 Failure)
*   **Symptom:** Solenoid "clicks" but the engine does not turn.
*   **Root Cause:** Failed battery charger or lack of "trickle charging."
*   **Expert Insight:** This is the most common reason for a Port State Control (PSC) detention.

## 4.2 Fuel Waxing / Stale Fuel
*   **Symptom:** Engine cranks but will not fire.
*   **Root Cause:** The engine is rarely used; the fuel sits in the tank for years, collecting water or "waxing" in cold climates.

## 4.3 Air Leaks in Suction Line
*   **Symptom:** Engine runs, but the pump never "primes."
*   **Root Cause:** The "Foot Valve" in the sea chest is leaking, or the suction pipe has developed a pinhole leak due to corrosion.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Cold Start" Test (SOLAS Requirement)
*   **Trade Trick:** You must be able to start the engine within 15 minutes at an ambient temp of 0°C. If your engine room is warm, test the batteries' **Cold Cranking Amps (CCA)** with a load tester to ensure it will work in winter.

## 5.2 The "Air Release" Valve
*   **Expert Insight:** There is a small air release valve on top of the pump casing. **Always keep this valve slightly open** when priming. If air is trapped in the "Eye" of the impeller, the pump will never establish suction.

## 5.3 Manual Fuel Priming
*   **Trade Trick:** Most emergency fire pump engines have a small "Hand Primer" on the fuel pump. If the engine has been sitting for a month, **pump this 50 times** before attempting to start to clear any air bubbles.

## 5.4 Using the "Hydraulic Start" Accumulator
*   **Trade Trick:** If you have a hydraulic start, ensure the pressure is at the maximum. If the pressure is low, **manually hand-pump the accumulator** until the gauge is in the green zone. This is a great workout and proves the "Black Start" capability.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine (Mandatory)
*   **Operational Test:** Start the engine and run the pump for at least 5 minutes. Verify water discharge from the nearest hydrant.
*   **Fuel Drain:** Open the tank drain valve slightly to remove any accumulated water.

## 6.2 Quarterly Battery Maintenance
*   **Method:** Check the electrolyte level in the lead-acid batteries. Clean the terminals and apply petroleum jelly to prevent corrosion.

## 6.3 Annual Pump Overhaul
*   **Check:** The "Priming Ejector" nozzle. If carbon soot from the exhaust blocks the ejector, the pump will never prime. Clean the nozzle with a wire brush.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **PSC Detention:** Failure of the emergency fire pump to start or prime during a Port State Control inspection is almost always an **immediate grounds for detention**.
*   **SOPEP Utility:** Like the main fire pump, the emergency pump is an essential tool for washing down decks after an oil spill if the engine room is evacuated.
