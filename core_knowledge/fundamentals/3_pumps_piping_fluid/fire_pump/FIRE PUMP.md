# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Firefighting Water

## 1.1 The Physics of Hydraulic Head and Throw
A fire pump must not only move volume but must also provide enough pressure (Head) to throw water at a fire from a safe distance.
*   **The Physics:** $P = \rho g h$
    The "Static Head" (height) of the ship must be overcome, plus the frictional losses in the piping, to maintain a working pressure at the highest hydrant (usually on the bridge or top deck).
*   **Jet Performance Rule:** Most class rules require that with two hydrants open, the pump must maintain a "Jet" of water reaching at least **12 meters** (approx. 40 feet) in any direction.

## 1.2 The Physics of "Jet" vs. "Spray"
Modern fire nozzles allow the operator to choose between a solid jet and a fine spray.
*   **The Physics:** The spray (mist) has a much larger **Surface Area** for the same volume of water. This increases the rate of heat absorption ($Q$), allowing the fire to be "cooled" more effectively than a solid stream.

## 1.3 Constant Pressure (The Fire Main)
*   **Physics:** The fire main is usually kept "charged" (pressurized) at all times. If a hydrant is opened, the pressure drops instantly.
*   **Auto-Start:** This drop in pressure is detected by a sensor that automatically starts the main fire pump.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 Main Fire Pumps
Usually at least two high-capacity centrifugal pumps.
*   **Redundancy:** They must be located in different compartments (e.g., Engine Room and Steering Gear Room) to ensure one survives if the other is lost to fire or flooding.

## 2.2 The Fire Main Piping
A large-diameter pipe that runs the entire length of the ship (forward to aft).
*   **Isolation Valves:** Critical valves that allow the engineer to "cut off" a damaged section of pipe (e.g., on a damaged deck) while keeping the rest of the main pressurized.

## 2.3 Hydrants and Hoses
*   **Hydrants:** Outlet valves located at strategic points.
*   **Hoses:** Usually 15–20 meters long, made of reinforced canvas or rubber.

## 2.4 International Shore Connection (ISC)
A standardized flange that allows the ship to receive water from a land-based fire truck or another vessel. **Physics:** It is a mandatory requirement that every ship has at least one ISC available on deck.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Standby:** Fire main pressure stable at 5.0 – 8.0 bar.
*   **Testing:** When a deck hydrant is opened, the pump starts automatically and maintains pressure even with multiple hoses running.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Main Pressure** | 5.0 – 9.0 bar | < 3.5 bar (Low Pressure) |
| **Pump Flow Rate** | Rated for vessel size | Low Flow (Pump Wear) |
| **Gland Leakage** | 2-3 drops / min | High Leakage (Packing Fail) |
| **Nozzle "Throw"** | > 12 meters | Short Throw (Blocked Filter) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Internal Corrosion (Pitting)
*   **Symptom:** Small "pinhole" leaks in the deck piping.
*   **Root Cause:** The fire main is often made of galvanized steel. If the galvanizing fails, saltwater causes rapid pitting.
*   **Expert Insight:** Often happens in "dead ends" of the pipe where water is stagnant.

## 4.2 Pump Failure to Prime
*   **Symptom:** Motor runs, but pressure does not rise.
*   **Root Cause:** Air leak in the suction sea chest or the "Self-Priming" air ejector is blocked.

## 4.3 Relief Valve Lifting (Chattering)
*   **Symptom:** Loud "clanging" from the pump; pressure fluctuates wildly.
*   **Root Cause:** All hydrants are closed while the pump is running, and the **Pressure Relief Valve (PRV)** is hunting.
*   **Expert Insight:** Ensure the PRV is set correctly to prevent "Water Hammer" from bursting the deck pipes.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Remote Start" Test
*   **Trade Trick:** Every week, test the pump from the **Bridge Remote Start** button. A pump that works locally in the ER but fails from the Bridge is a major safety deficiency.

## 5.2 Checking Hydrant "SDNR" Function
*   **Trade Trick:** Every fire hydrant should be a **Screw-Down Non-Return (SDNR)** valve. To test, open the hydrant with no hose connected while the main is pressurized. If water "surges" and then stops, the non-return disc is working.

## 5.3 The "Gland Packing" Adjustment
*   **Expert Insight:** Fire pumps are used intermittently. If you tighten the gland packing too much while cold, the shaft will "seize" when the pump runs for a long period and the shaft expands from heat. **Always allow for a slight drip.**

## 5.4 Using the "ISC" to Pressure Test
*   **Trade Trick:** During drydock, use the International Shore Connection to pressurize the fire main using the yard's water supply. This allows you to find leaks without running the ship's pumps.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine
*   **Physical Inspection:** Inspect all fire hoses for dry-rot and ensure all nozzles move freely from "Jet" to "Spray."
*   **Valve Greasing:** Grease the spindles of all deck hydrants. If they are exposed to salt spray, they will "seize" within weeks.

## 6.2 Annual Pressure Test
*   **Method:** Pressurize the entire main to 1.5x the working pressure (approx. 12 bar) for 30 minutes. Any weak spots or "thinned" pipes will fail, allowing for repair *before* an actual fire.

## 6.3 Pump Overhaul
*   **Check:** Inspect the "Wear Rings." If the gap between the impeller and the housing is too large, the pump will not be able to achieve the mandatory "12-meter throw."

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Jockey Pump:** Some ships use a small "Jockey Pump" to maintain the fire main pressure, preventing the large Main Fire Pump from starting every time there is a tiny leak.
*   **SOPEP Utility:** In the event of a minor oil spill on deck, the fire hose is the primary tool for washing the oil into the scuppers (assuming the scuppers are plugged!).
