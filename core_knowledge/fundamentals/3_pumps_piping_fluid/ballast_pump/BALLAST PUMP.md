# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Ballasting

## 1.1 The Physics of Ship Stability (Metacentric Height)
The primary role of the ballast system is to maintain the ship's stability and trim by adjusting the mass of water in the hull.
*   **The Physics:** Ballasting lowers the ship's **Center of Gravity** ($G$), thereby increasing the **Metacentric Height** ($GM$). A larger $GM$ results in a more stable ship with a stronger "Righting Lever" ($GZ$).
*   **Physics of Trim:** By moving water between Forward and Aft tanks, the engineer can adjust the ship's "Trim" to optimize fuel efficiency or ensure sufficient propeller immersion.

## 1.2 The "Free Surface Effect" Physics
A partially full ballast tank is dangerous.
*   **The Physics:** When the ship rolls, the water in a partially full tank "sloshes" to the low side, shifting the center of gravity laterally ($G$ moves to $G'$). This reduces the effective $GM$, potentially leading to a capsize in heavy weather.
*   **Rule:** Ballast tanks should either be **empty** or **pressed-up** (100% full) to eliminate this effect.

## 1.3 Ballast Water Treatment (The Regulatory Physics)
The IMO Ballast Water Management Convention (BWM Convention) requires that water be treated before discharge to prevent the spread of invasive species.
*   **Physics of Treatment:** Most BWTS use **UV Irradiation** (destroying DNA) or **Electro-chlorination** (creating active substances that kill microorganisms) combined with 20–40 micron filtration.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Ballast Pump
Usually a high-capacity centrifugal pump.
*   **Suction:** Connected to both the "High" and "Low" sea chests.
*   **Discharge:** Connected to the Main Ballast Manifold.

## 2.2 The Ballast Manifold
A centralized valve chest that allows water to be pumped *into* any tank (ballasting), *out of* any tank (de-ballasting), or *transferred* between tanks.

## 2.3 Air Pipes and Air Heads
Every ballast tank has an air pipe to allow air to escape (filling) or enter (emptying).
*   **Component:** The "Air Head" contains a floating ball that seals the pipe if a wave hits it, preventing seawater from entering the tank.

## 2.4 Tank Gauging System
Uses radar or hydrostatic pressure sensors to provide real-time level data in the ECR.

## 2.5 The Eductor (Stripping)
*   **Physics:** A small Venturi-style pump used to "strip" the last 10–20cm of water from a tank that the main centrifugal pump cannot reach due to air-drawing.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Flow Rate:** Constant and matching the pump's performance curve (e.g., 500 – 1500 m³/h).
*   **Tank Level:** Rising or falling smoothly at a predictable rate.
*   **BWTS:** All parameters (UV intensity, Tro/TRC levels) within the "Approved Type" limits.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pump Discharge Press** | 2.5 – 4.5 bar | > 6.0 bar (Blocked Line) |

| **Tank Sounding Accuracy** | ± 1-2 cm | High Deviation |
| **BWTS Filter ΔP** | < 0.3 bar | > 0.6 bar (Auto-Backwash) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Tank Over-Pressurization (The "Burst" Risk)
*   **Symptom:** Structural deformation of the deck or tank top; water spraying from manhole covers.
*   **Root Cause:** Pumping into a full tank with a blocked air pipe.
*   **Physics:** Water is incompressible. If the pump continues to push water into a closed space, the internal pressure will exceed the hull's yield strength.

## 4.2 Pump Cavitation (Drawing Air)
*   **Symptom:** Discharge pressure fluctuates; loud "gravel" sound from the pump.
*   **Root Cause:** Trying to pump out a tank that is nearly empty, or a "vortex" forming at the suction bell-mouth.

## 4.3 BWTS Filter Clogging
*   **Symptom:** Frequent auto-backwashing; discharge flow rate drops to zero.
*   **Root Cause:** Bunkering or de-ballasting in "brown water" (silt-heavy river water).

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Gravity Ballasting" Trick
*   **Trade Trick:** If the tanks are empty and the ship is deep in the water, you can "Gravity Ballast" by opening the sea inlet and the tank valves without starting the pump. This saves fuel and pump wear.

## 5.2 Clearing a Blocked Air Head
*   **Expert Insight:** If a tank is "struggling" to fill, the ball in the air head may be stuck in the up position. **Hit the air head housing with a rubber mallet** to drop the ball. If the ball is stuck, the tank can literally "implode" during de-ballasting.

## 5.3 Determining "Structural Integrity" via Sounding
*   **Trade Trick:** If a tank's "Sounding" (level) increases without ballasting, you have a hull crack or a leaking valve from an adjacent tank. Perform a **Pressure Test** to confirm.

## 5.4 Using the "Eductor" Correctly
*   **Expert Insight:** The eductor requires high-pressure "Driving Water" (usually from the fire pump). **Ensure the driving water pressure is at least 3x the discharge head** for maximum stripping efficiency.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Monthly Routine
*   **Valve Exercise:** Operate all ballast manifold valves (even remote ones) to prevent them from "seizing" in the open or closed position.
*   **Air Head Screen:** Check the mesh screen on the air pipes for salt/rust buildup.

## 6.2 Pump Impeller Inspection
*   **Check:** Ballast pumps move a lot of sand and silt. Inspect the impeller every 2 years. If the "Clearance" between the impeller and the wear rings is > 1.5mm, the pump will lose 30% of its efficiency.

## 6.3 Tank Coating Inspection
*   **Check:** Ballast tanks are "WFT" (Water Ballast Tank) coated. If the coating fails, the ship's structure will corrode rapidly. Check for "Blistering" or "Red Rust."

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **BWM Record Book:** Every ballast operation (Start, Stop, Location, Volume, and Treatment Method) must be recorded. It is as legally important as the Oil Record Book.
*   **D-2 Standard:** The current IMO standard for "Discharge Quality," requiring less than 10 viable organisms (> 50 microns) per cubic meter.
