# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Emergency Escape

## 1.1 The Purpose of EEBD (SOLAS Chapter II-2)
An EEBD is a life-saving device designed strictly for **escaping** from a compartment that has a hazardous atmosphere (smoke, toxic gasses, or oxygen deficiency).
*   **The Physics:** Unlike an SCBA, an EEBD is not meant for working or firefighting. It provides a constant flow of breathable air or oxygen to allow the user to reach the open deck.
*   **Duration Physics:** SOLAS requires a minimum duration of **10 minutes**. This is calculated based on the average time needed to escape from the deepest part of the Engine Room to the safety of the boat deck.

## 1.2 Constant Flow vs. Lung Demand Physics
1.  **Constant Flow (Compressed Air):** 
    *   **The Physics:** A small cylinder (e.g., 2 liters at 200 bar) releases air at a fixed rate (approx. 35–40 lpm). 
    *   **Advantage:** Simpler mechanism; easier to put on (usually a "Hood" rather than a tight mask).
2.  **Oxygen Generation (Chemical):** 
    *   **The Physics:** Uses $KO_2$ (Potassium Superoxide). The wearer's exhaled moisture and $CO_2$ react with the chemical to release Oxygen.
    *   **Advantage:** Extremely compact and light; has a longer shelf life.

## 1.3 The Physics of the "Hood"
*   **The Physics:** The EEBD usually features a flame-resistant, high-visibility hood with a clear visor. 
*   **Air-curtain Effect:** The constant flow of air into the hood creates a slight positive pressure and a "curtain" of fresh air around the face, preventing smoke from entering the neck seal.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 Compressed Air Cylinder (or Chemical Canister)
*   **Cylinder:** High-pressure steel or aluminum. 
*   **Pressure Gauge:** Visible through a window in the carrying bag so it can be checked without opening the unit.

## 2.2 Pressure Reducer and Hose
Reduces the cylinder pressure to a breathable level and delivers it to the hood.

## 2.3 The Escape Hood
*   **Neck Seal:** Elasticated seal to prevent smoke ingress.
*   **Inner Mask/Nose Cup:** Reduces $CO_2$ buildup and prevents fogging of the visor.

## 2.4 Carrying Bag and Harness
High-visibility orange or yellow bag with clear instructions printed on the side. **Physics:** Designed for "Donning" in less than **10 seconds**.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Pressure Gauge:** Needle is in the "Green" zone (Full).
*   **Tamper Seal:** The plastic security seal on the bag is intact.
*   **Expiry Date:** Both the unit and the hydrostatic test date are within the valid range.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cylinder Pressure** | 200 bar | < 180 bar (Refill/Replace) |
| **Duration** | 10 – 15 minutes | < 10 mins (Non-Compliant) |
| **Donning Time** | < 10 seconds | Slow Donning (Safety Risk) |
| **Shelf Life** | 10 – 15 years | Expired (Replace) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Pressure Loss (The Slow Leak)
*   **Symptom:** Gauge needle is in the "Red" zone.
*   **Root Cause:** Failed O-ring in the regulator or a leaking safety bursting disc.
*   **Action:** Unit must be sent ashore for refilling and servicing.

## 4.2 Cracked Visor
*   **Symptom:** Small "Spider-web" cracks in the hood plastic.
*   **Root Cause:** Prolonged exposure to high temperatures or UV light in the Engine Room.
*   **Danger:** The visor may shatter during the escape, allowing smoke into the eyes.

## 4.3 Seized Automatic Activation
*   **Symptom:** The bag is opened, but the air doesn't start flowing.
*   **Root Cause:** The pull-pin mechanism is corroded or salt-encrusted.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Visual" Only Rule
*   **Trade Trick:** Never "Test" an EEBD by opening the bag. **Expert Insight:** Most EEBDs are **one-time use**. Once the bag is opened and the seal is broken, the air begins to flow and the unit is exhausted. **Only open an EEBD in a real emergency.**

## 5.2 Checking the "Hydrostatic" Date
*   **Trade Trick:** Don't just look at the bag. Look at the date stamped on the **Cylinder Neck**. **Expert Insight:** Ships often change the bags but forget to test the cylinders. A cylinder more than 5 years old without a test stamp is a "Safety Deficiency."

## 5.3 The "Humidity Indicator" (Chemical Type)
*   **Trade Trick:** Chemical EEBDs have a small "Window" with a color-changing bead (usually Blue). **If the bead turns Pink**, the canister has absorbed moisture and the chemical is dead. Replace the unit immediately.

## 5.4 Strategic Placement
*   **Expert Insight:** EEBDs must be located along the "Primary Escape Routes." **Trade Trick:** Ensure one is located near the **Engine Control Room (ECR)** and one in each **Generator Room**. Do not hide them behind spare parts!

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine (Mandatory)
*   **Visual Check:** Walk the escape routes and verify every EEBD gauge is in the green. 
*   **Accessibility:** Ensure the units are not obstructed by tools or debris.

## 6.2 Monthly Training
*   **Method:** Use a **"Training EEBD"** (which is reusable and empty) to train the crew on donning procedures. **Safety Goal:** Every crew member must be able to put on the hood and start moving within 10 seconds while blinded (to simulate smoke).

## 6.3 Annual Inspection
*   **Requirement:** Verification by the Safety Officer or an authorized service provider. Check for any "Manufacturer Recalls" (Common with certain EEBD brands).

---

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Prohibited Use:** Never use an EEBD for:
    1.  Entering a tank for inspection.
    2.  Fighting a fire.
    3.  Rescuing a casualty.
*   **Spare Units:** SOLAS requires at least **two spare EEBDs** to be carried on board.
