# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Inflatable Survival

## 1.1 The Physics of Inflatable Buoyancy
A liferaft must provide stable buoyancy for up to 25+ people in extreme sea states.
*   **The Physics:** Uses independent buoyancy chambers (usually two). 
*   **Redundancy:** If one chamber is punctured, the remaining chamber must have enough volume to keep all occupants afloat.
*   **Insulation Physics:** The raft features a double-skinned floor that can be inflated. **Physics:** This creates an air-gap that prevents the occupants' body heat from being sucked away by the cold ocean (Hypothermia protection).

## 1.2 Inflation Physics (CO2/N2 Mix)
*   **The Physics:** The raft is inflated by a pressurized cylinder containing a mix of $CO_2$ and Nitrogen. 
*   **Phase Change:** The $CO_2$ provides the volume, while the Nitrogen prevents the $CO_2$ from freezing during the rapid expansion (Joule-Thomson effect).
*   **Speed:** A standard raft must fully inflate within **60 seconds** at $20^\circ C$.

## 1.3 Stability and the Water Pockets
*   **The Problem:** A light, inflated raft is easily flipped by wind or waves.
*   **The Physics Solution:** **Ballast Pockets (Water Pockets)**. These are large bags under the raft that automatically fill with seawater. **Physics:** They add "Virtual Mass" and create a vacuum-seal against the water surface, making the raft extremely difficult to capsize.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Container (Cradle)
A GRP (Glass Reinforced Plastic) shell that protects the raft from UV light, salt, and physical damage.
*   **Physics:** Designed to break open easily when the raft starts to inflate.

## 2.2 The Painter and Static Line
*   **Painter:** A long rope (usually 15–30 meters) that connects the raft to the ship.
*   **Static Line:** The part of the painter that triggers the $CO_2$ cylinder when pulled to its full length.

## 2.3 Hydrostatic Release Unit (HRU)
The "Mechanical Mind" of the raft.
*   **The Physics:** A spring-loaded knife held back by a water-pressure diaphragm.
*   **Trigger:** At a depth of **2–4 meters**, the pressure triggers the knife, cutting the securing strap and allowing the raft to float free.

## 2.4 Survival Equipment (The Pack)
Contains rations, water, flares, medical supplies, and a sea anchor. **Physics:** The sea anchor (Drogue) is critical for reducing "Drift" and keeping the raft's entry door pointed away from the wind.

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 "Float-Free" Failure
*   **Symptom:** Ship sinks, but the raft stays attached to the cradle.
*   **Root Cause:** The painter was tied directly to the ship's railing instead of the **Weak Link**.
*   **Physics:** Without the weak link, the raft is dragged down with the ship.

## 4.2 RAFT Inflation Failure
*   **Symptom:** Container opens but raft remains flat.
*   **Root Cause:** $CO_2$ cylinder has leaked or the firing pin is corroded.

## 4.3 Container Damage (Water Ingress)
*   **Symptom:** Container feels heavy; water dripping from the bottom.
*   **Root Cause:** Improperly sealed GRP halves or physical impact from a crane.
*   **Danger:** If the raft fabric sits in saltwater for months, it will rot and fail to inflate.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Weak Link" Logic (The 1-2-3 Check)
*   **Critical Knowledge:** You must understand the 3 connections of a liferaft:
    1.  **Strong Connection:** The raft strap to the HRU (Cuts at 4m).
    2.  **Painter Connection:** The raft rope to the ship (Used to trigger inflation).
    3.  **Weak Link:** The tiny plastic/rope piece that connects the painter to the ship. **Physics:** It must break at a force of approx. **2.2 kN**. If the ship sinks, the buoyancy of the inflated raft snaps this link, allowing the raft to stay on the surface.

## 5.2 Correct Painter Attachment
*   **Trade Trick:** Ensure the painter is not "Slack" but not "Tight." **Expert Insight:** If the painter is too loose, it might get tangled in the ship's railings during a real emergency.

## 5.3 Detecting a "Tampered" HRU
*   **Trade Trick:** Look at the plastic "Nut" on the HRU. **Expert Insight:** Many crew members tighten this nut with a wrench. **Warning:** The HRU nut must be **hand-tight only**. If it is too tight, the internal spring cannot move, and the raft will never release.

## 5.4 Using the Sea Anchor (Drogue)
*   **Expert Insight:** In a storm, the raft will move at 3–5 knots with the wind. **Trade Trick:** Deploy the sea anchor immediately. **Physics:** It creates massive drag in the water, slowing the drift and preventing the raft from flipping in high winds.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine
*   **Visual:** Verify the "Weak Link" is present and the painter is secured.
*   **Seal Check:** Ensure the "Tamper Seals" on the container are intact.

## 6.2 Annual Service (Mandatory)
*   **Requirement:** Liferafts must be sent to an **Authorized Service Station** every 12 months.
*   **Tasks:** The raft is inflated, the $CO_2$ cylinder is weighed, the food/water is replaced, and a "Pressure Test" is performed. **Certification:** The "Liferaft Service Certificate" is a mandatory item for the Safety Equipment Certificate.

## 6.3 HRU Replacement
*   **Method:** Replace the HRU every 2 years. **Safety:** Never attempt to repair an HRU; it is a factory-sealed unit. **Marking:** Punch the "Year and Month" of expiry on the HRU label before installing.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Davit-Launched Rafts:** These are inflated on deck, the crew boards, and then the raft is lowered to the water by a crane. **Physics:** Requires a much stronger raft structure to handle the weight while suspended.
*   **Hydrostatic vs. Manual:** In a quick sinking (e.g., collision), you won't have time to throw the raft. Trust the **Float-free** physics of the HRU.
