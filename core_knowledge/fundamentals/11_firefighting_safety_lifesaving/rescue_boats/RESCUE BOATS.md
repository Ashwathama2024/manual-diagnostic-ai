# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Rapid Rescue

## 1.1 The Physics of Speed and Maneuverability
Unlike a lifeboat (which is for evacuation), a rescue boat is for **Action**.
*   **The Physics:** It must be capable of a speed of at least **6 knots** for at least 4 hours. Fast Rescue Boats (FRB) must reach **20 knots**.
*   **Planing Physics:** RHIBs use a V-shaped hull to "Plane" on top of the water. **Physics:** Reduces drag ($D$) and increases speed ($V$), allowing the boat to reach a person in the water (PIW) before hypothermia sets in.

## 1.2 Towing Physics
A rescue boat must be able to tow the ship's largest liferaft when fully loaded.
*   **The Physics:** Force $T = D_{raft} + D_{towline}$. 
*   **Requirement:** The boat must be able to tow a loaded liferaft at a speed of at least **2 knots** in calm water. This requires high-torque propeller/waterjet characteristics.

## 1.3 Launching Physics (Single-Point Suspension)
Rescue boats are usually launched via a **Single-Arm Davit**.
*   **The Physics:** The boat is suspended from a single central hook. This allows for extremely rapid launching (less than **5 minutes**).
*   **Anti-Pendulation:** To prevent the boat from swinging like a pendulum as the ship rolls, the davit uses a "Constant Tension" winch or "V-shaped" cradles.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Boat Structure
*   **Hull:** Rigid GRP or Aluminum.
*   **Sponsons:** Inflatable buoyancy tubes made of Hypalon or PVC. **Physics:** Provides extreme stability and "fendering" when coming alongside a person in the water.

## 2.2 Propulsion (Outboard vs. Inboard Waterjet)
1.  **Outboard Engine:** Usually gasoline (petrol) or diesel. **Physics:** Offers excellent maneuverability by vectored thrust.
2.  **Inboard Waterjet:** **Physics:** Safer for people in the water (no exposed propeller). It uses a pump to accelerate water through a nozzle.

## 2.3 Single-Point Hook (Automatic Release)
A specialized hook (e.g., Henriksen or Schat-Harding) that allows for manual or hydrostatic release.

## 2.4 Righting Frame
An A-frame or "Roll Bar" at the stern. **Physics:** Contains an inflatable bag. If the boat flips, the crew can trigger the bag to flip the boat back upright.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Sponsons:** Firm to the touch. No "Hissing" sounds.
*   **Engine:** "Pisses" water from the cooling indicator (for outboards). Instant throttle response.
*   **Davit:** The hydraulic oil level is stable; no leaks from the crane arm.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Speed (Min)** | 6 Knots (20 for FRB) | < 6 Knots (Fouled Prop) |
| **Sponson Pressure** | 0.15 – 0.25 bar | < 0.1 bar (Soft/Unstable) |
| **Battery Voltage** | 12.5 V – 14.0 V | < 11.5 V (No Start) |
| **Fuel Capacity** | 100% | < 50% (Refill) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Sponson Deflation (The "Soft" Boat)
*   **Symptom:** The boat feels unstable and "digs in" during turns.
*   **Root Cause:** Puncture from a sharp object on the ship's side or a leaking "Topping-up" valve.
*   **Expert Insight:** Often caused by over-inflation in the sun, which causes the pressure-relief valves to pop.

## 4.2 Outboard Engine "Cutting Out"
*   **Symptom:** Engine starts but dies when throttle is applied.
*   **Root Cause:** Water in the fuel or a blocked "Fuel Tank Vent."
*   **Physics:** If the vent is closed, the pump creates a vacuum in the tank, and fuel flow stops.

## 4.3 Single Hook Seizure
*   **Symptom:** The boat is in the water, but the hook won't release.
*   **Root Cause:** Salt-water corrosion of the release cam.
*   **Danger:** A surging boat attached to a fixed ship will be pulled underwater or ripped apart.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Kill-Cord" Check (The #1 Engine Fault)
*   **Trade Trick:** If the engine won't fire, check the **Emergency Stop Kill-Cord**. **Expert Insight:** 50% of "Broken" rescue boat engines are simply missing the plastic clip on the kill-switch.

## 5.2 Starting a "Drowned" Outboard
*   **Trade Trick:** If the boat has been swamped and the engine is wet, remove the spark plugs, turn the engine over 5 times to "spit" out the water, spray WD-40 into the cylinders, and try again. **Expert Insight:** Do this within 30 minutes, or the internal bearings will rust.

## 5.3 Correct Sponson Inflation
*   **Trade Trick:** Never inflate sponsons to "Rock Hard" in the morning. **Physics:** As the sun heats the air, the pressure will double. Inflate to "Firm" only. **Expert Insight:** Use a pressure gauge; "Thumping" the tube with your hand is not a test.

## 5.4 Using the "Painter" for Stability
*   **Expert Insight:** When launching in heavy weather, the **Painter** (the front rope) is your most important tool. **Trade Trick:** Keep the painter tight until the boat is fully in the water and the hook is released. This keeps the boat's bow pointed into the waves and prevents it from spinning.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine (Mandatory)
*   **Engine Run:** Run the engine for at least 3 minutes (use a "Flush-muff" for outboards to provide cooling water).
*   **Inventory:** Ensure the VHF radio, searchlight, and medical kit are present.

## 6.2 Monthly Davit Test
*   **Method:** Swing the davit out to the "Launch Position" (without lowering the boat to the water). Check for hydraulic "Creep" in the crane arm.

## 6.3 Annual Inspection
*   **Requirement:** Thorough examination of the boat's hull, sponsons, and engine. **Hydrostatic Test:** The fuel tank and air bottles (if fitted) must be checked for leaks.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Gasoline Safety:** If the boat uses gasoline (petrol), the fuel must be stored in an approved "Fire-proof" locker on the open deck. **Warning:** Gasoline vapors are heavier than air and will explode if they leak into the boat's hull.
*   **Recovery:** Recovering a rescue boat in high seas is the most dangerous part of the operation. The helmsman must time the "Hook-up" at the **Peak of a Wave** to minimize the shock load on the davit.
