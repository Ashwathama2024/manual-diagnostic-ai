# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Service Air

## 1.1 The Physics of Bulk Air Demand
Working air is used for "high-volume, low-precision" tasks like deck scaling, soot blowing, and pneumatic cleaning.
*   **The Physics:** Unlike control air, working air systems are designed for high **Volumetric Flow Rates** $(SCFM/m^3/h)$ rather than high pressure or extreme dryness.
*   **Pressure Physics:** Usually maintained at **7.0 – 8.5 bar**. This is the standard operating pressure for most industrial pneumatic tools (grinders, impact wrenches).

## 1.2 The Physics of Air Storage (The Receiver)
The working air receiver acts as a "Pneumatic Battery."
*   **The Physics:** $V_{tank} = \frac{T \cdot (C - F) \cdot P_{atm}}{P_1 - P_2}$
    Where $T$ is the time, $C$ is compressor capacity, and $F$ is air demand. 
*   **Buffer Effect:** The receiver prevents the compressor from starting every time a deck-hand uses a small blow-gun. It also allows the air to "rest," which helps water and oil droplets to settle by gravity.

## 1.3 Condensation Physics in Distribution
Working air is often sent through long pipe runs on the open deck.
*   **The Problem:** As the warm air cools down in the deck pipes (especially in winter), water condenses inside the pipes.
*   **Result:** This leads to "Slugging" (water hitting pneumatic tools) and internal corrosion of the pipes.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Compressor Unit
Usually a rotary screw compressor, similar to the control air set, but often larger or configured for higher durability in harsh environments.

## 2.2 The Service Air Receiver
A large pressure vessel located in the engine room or on a sheltered deck.
*   **Fittings:** High-capacity drain valve, pressure relief valve, and a main isolation valve.

## 2.3 The Air Line Oiler (Lubricator)
*   **Function:** Injects a fine mist of oil into the air line for specific branches (e.g., for the Engine Room workshop tools). 
*   **Physics:** Prevents the internal vanes of pneumatic motors from seizing due to friction.

## 2.4 Deck Service Stations
Points on the deck where hoses can be connected. Each station typically has a filter/regulator and a moisture trap.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Sound:** A steady, muffled hum from the screw unit. No high-pitched "Whistles" (which indicate air leaks).
*   **Recovery:** When a large tool is used (like a pneumatic scaler), the compressor should start smoothly and rebuild pressure quickly.
*   **Drainage:** Regular "puffs" from the auto-drains.

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **System Pressure** | 7.0 – 8.5 bar | < 6.0 bar (Low Flow) |
| **Compressor Outlet Temp**| 80°C – 100°C | > 110°C (High Temp Trip) |
| **Receiver Level (Water)** | < 5% of Volume | > 20% (Water Slugging) |
| **Filter ΔP** | < 0.5 bar | > 1.0 bar (Clogged) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Massive Air Leaks (The "Invisible" Loss)
*   **Symptom:** Compressor runs 24/7 even when no one is using air.
*   **Root Cause:** Corroded deck pipes, "forgotten" open valves on the mast, or leaking hose couplings.
*   **Physics:** A 1mm hole at 7 bar can waste over **2,000 liters of air per hour**, leading to significant fuel waste.

## 4.2 Water Slugging at Tools
*   **Symptom:** Grinders and wrenches "spit" water and lose power.
*   **Root Cause:** Failure to drain the main receiver or the lack of "Drip Legs" in the distribution piping.

## 4.3 Compressor Overheating
*   **Symptom:** "High Temperature" alarm; oil smell in the air.
*   **Root Cause:** Clogged cooling fins on the radiator or low oil level.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Ultrasonic" Leak Hunt
*   **Trade Trick:** In a noisy engine room, you cannot hear air leaks. Use an **Ultrasonic Leak Detector**. It translates the high-frequency "hiss" of a leak into an audible beep in your headphones. You can find a tiny leak from 10 meters away.

## 5.2 Detecting a Failing "Separator"
*   **Expert Insight:** If you see "Yellow Sludge" in your deck service stations, your compressor's **Air/Oil Separator Element** is ruptured. **Stop the compressor immediately.** The oil is being "pumped" into the air lines, creating a fire risk and destroying your tools.

## 5.3 The "Ice-Pick" Drain Clear
*   **Trade Trick:** Working air receiver drains often get blocked with "Rust Flakes." If you open the drain valve and nothing happens, **DO NOT stick a wire up the pipe while it is pressurized.** Instead, use a "Purge Valve" or hit the pipe gently with a hammer to vibrate the scales loose.

## 5.4 Using Working Air for "Starting"
*   **Expert Insight:** In an emergency, the working air system can sometimes be cross-connected to the **Starting Air system** via a small jumper hose. **Warning:** Working air is only at 7 bar; it will NOT start the main engine, but it might be enough to start a small **Emergency Generator** or provide "Control Air" to the safety system.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Weekly Routine
*   **Manual Blow-down:** Open all "Low Point" drains on the deck air main.
*   **Filter Check:** Clean the intake air filters.

## 6.2 Annual Receiver Inspection
*   **Requirement:** The working air receiver must be inspected for **Internal Pitting**. Since service air is "Wet," the bottom of the receiver is prone to rapid corrosion. **Check the thickness of the bottom plate** using an Ultrasonic meter.

## 6.3 Screw Unit Oil Change
*   **Method:** Every 4000 hours (approx. 6 months). Always use **screw-specific oil**. Standard reciprocating oil will foam and cause the compressor to "vomit" oil into the air lines.

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Pneumatic Tools Safety:** Always use a "Safety Cable" (Whip-check) on all air hose connections. If a hose snaps at 7 bar, it becomes a lethal "Whip."
*   **Sea Chest Venting:** Working air is often used to "blow out" the sea chests to clear seaweed or ice. Ensure the valve is only opened momentarily to prevent high-pressure air from entering the cooling pumps.
