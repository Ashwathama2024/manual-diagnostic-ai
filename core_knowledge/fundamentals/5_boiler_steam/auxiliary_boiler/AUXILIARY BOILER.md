# Part 1 – Fundamentals & Physics of Steam Generation

## 1.1 The Physics of Phase Change (Latent Heat)
A boiler converts water (liquid) into steam (gas) by adding thermal energy.
*   **The Physics:** $Q = m \cdot L$
    Once water reaches its boiling point ($100^\circ C$ at 1 bar, approx. $170^\circ C$ at 7 bar), the temperature stops rising, and all added energy (Latent Heat of Vaporization) goes into breaking the molecular bonds to create steam.
*   **Energy Density:** Steam is a highly efficient "Heat Carrier" because it releases this massive Latent Heat when it condenses back to water at the destination (e.g., fuel heaters).

## 1.2 Combustion Physics
The burner must mix fuel and air in the correct ratio (Stoichiometric Ratio).
*   **Too Much Air:** Increases "Stack Losses" (heat wasted out the funnel).
*   **Too Little Air:** Causes "Incomplete Combustion," producing black smoke and "Soot" which insulates the tubes and creates a fire risk in the uptake.

## 1.3 Natural Circulation Physics
In most marine auxiliary boilers (Water-tube type), the water circulates naturally.
*   **The Physics:** Hotter water/steam mixture has a lower density and rises (Risers), while cooler water sinks (Downcomers). This "Thermo-siphon" effect ensures the tubes are always cooled by water, preventing them from melting.

# Part 2 – Major Components & System Layout

## 2.1 The Furnace and Pressure Parts
*   **Furnace:** The "Firebox" where combustion occurs. Lined with "Refractory" bricks to protect the steel shell from direct flame.
*   **Steam Drum:** The upper vessel where steam separates from the water.
*   **Water Drum:** The lower vessel that acts as a reservoir for the circulating water.

## 2.2 The Burner Assembly
*   **Atomizer:** Breaks the fuel into a fine mist using pressure or "Atomizing Steam."
*   **Forced Draught (FD) Fan:** Provides the oxygen for combustion.
*   **Flame Scanner:** An optical sensor (UV or IR) that monitors the flame. **Safety:** If the flame disappears, the scanner shuts off the fuel instantly (MFT - Master Fuel Trip).

## 2.3 Safety Valves (The Ultimate Protection)
*   **Function:** Every boiler must have at least **two** safety valves.
*   **Physics:** They are spring-loaded valves that pop open if the steam pressure exceeds the design limit. They must be able to discharge 100% of the boiler's capacity to prevent an explosion.

## 2.4 Gauge Glass (The Eye)
A high-pressure glass tube that allows the engineer to physically see the water level. It is the only "Truth" in boiler operation; electronic sensors are secondary.

# Part 3 – Normal Operation & Key Parameters

## 3.1 What "Healthy" Looks Like
*   **Flame:** Bright yellow or blue (depending on fuel), consistent shape, no "touching" the furnace walls.
*   **Water Level:** Steady at 50% in the gauge glass. 
*   **Steam Quality:** "Dry" steam (no water droplets carried over).

## 3.2 Typical Operating Parameters

| Parameter | Healthy Value | Alarm / Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Steam Pressure** | 7.0 – 9.0 bar | > 10.0 bar (Safety Lift) |
| **Water Level** | 40% – 60% | < 25% (Low Level Trip) |
| **Stack Temperature** | 220°C – 280°C | > 350°C (Uptake Fire Risk) |
| **TDS (Total Solids)** | < 2000 ppm | > 3000 ppm (Priming Risk) |
| **Phosphate (PO4)** | 20 – 40 ppm | < 10 ppm (Scale Risk) |

# Part 4 – Common Faults & Root Causes

## 4.1 Low Water Level (The "Glow" Disaster)
*   **Symptom:** "Low-Low Water" alarm; burner trips.
*   **Root Cause:** Feed pump failure or a stuck-open blow-down valve.
*   **Physics:** If the tubes are not covered by water, the furnace heat will raise the steel temperature until it reaches its "Plastic State" and collapses. **NEVER add water to a dry, hot boiler; it will explode.**

## 4.2 Priming and Carry-Over
*   **Symptom:** Water "hammer" in the steam pipes; sudden drop in steam pressure.
*   **Root Cause:** High TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) or high alkalinity causing "Foaming" on the water surface.
*   **Physics:** The foam "sucks" water droplets into the steam outlet.

## 4.3 Burner Flame-Out
*   **Symptom:** Burner stops; "Flame Failure" alarm.
*   **Root Cause:** Dirty atomizer, water in the fuel, or a fouled flame scanner lens.

# Part 5 – Expert Troubleshooting & Trade Tricks

## 5.1 The "Blow-Down" Procedure (Surface vs. Bottom)
*   **Surface Blow-down:** Used to remove floating oils and scum from the water surface.
*   **Bottom Blow-down:** Used to remove heavy "Sludge" and "Mud" from the bottom of the boiler.
*   **Trade Trick:** To lower the TDS quickly, perform a 5-second "Bottom Blow" every 15 minutes rather than one long blow. This is more effective at "disturbing" the sludge.

## 5.2 Checking the Gauge Glass "Accuracy"
*   **Trade Trick:** Perform a **"Blow-through"** of the gauge glass. 
    *   **Method:** Close the water and steam cocks and open the drain. Then open them one by one. If the water doesn't return to the level instantly, the small "orifice" in the boiler shell is blocked with scale.

## 5.3 Cleaning the Flame Scanner
*   **Trade Trick:** If the burner trips on "Flame Fail" but the flame looks perfect, **wipe the scanner lens with a soft cloth.** A thin film of soot on the lens is enough to "blind" the sensor.

## 5.4 Detecting Tube Leaks via "Consumption"
*   **Expert Insight:** If your Feed Water consumption increases significantly but you are not "blowing down," you have a **Tube Leak**. The water is evaporating and going up the funnel with the exhaust gasses.

# Part 6 – Maintenance & Preventive Checks

## 6.1 Boiler Water Treatment (Chemicals)
*   **Alkalinity Control:** Keeps pH between 10.5 and 11.5 to prevent acid corrosion.
*   **Phosphate:** Reacts with calcium to form a soft sludge instead of hard scale.
*   **Oxygen Scavenger (Hydrazine/Sulfite):** Removes dissolved oxygen to prevent "Pitting" corrosion.

## 6.2 The "Accumulation Test" (Annual)
*   **Method:** Close all steam outlets, run the burner at full capacity, and verify that the safety valves can maintain the pressure below 110% of design pressure.

## 6.3 Fire-Side Cleaning
*   **Method:** Every 6 months, open the burner door and "Soot-Blow" or manually scrape the soot from the tubes. **Soot is a better insulator than asbestos; 1mm of soot can reduce efficiency by 10%.**

# Part 7 – Miscellaneous Knowledge

*   **Shore Steam:** In some drydocks, the ship's boiler is shut down, and steam is provided from a land-based pipe. **Careful:** Ensure the "Non-Return" valve is working to prevent your boiler from being accidentally filled by the shore.
*   **Hot Standby:** To prevent "Thermal Stress" on the bricks (refractory), the boiler should be kept warm even when not in use.
